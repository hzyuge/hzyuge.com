![Starting our ride at the park](images/opening.jpg)

The weekend sun spills lazily over the entrance to the cycling park. My little guy and I each wheel our bikes through the gate, in no particular hurry, stepping onto the rainbow-colored bike path. There is no destination, no schedule, not even a plan for how many laps we might do. The only thing on today's agenda is to slow down.

He pedals ahead, glancing back every few seconds. "Dad, look! A butterfly — a really pretty one!" He lunges forward with a burst of pedaling, but before he gets anywhere close, the butterfly is long gone. He doesn't seem to mind. Grinning, he circles back to me, his tires tracing colorful lines on the pavement like brushstrokes in a painting meant only for this afternoon.

No need to rush. Just ride. It sounds simple, but if you've spent any time being a grown-up, you know that "slowing down" is a skill you have to relearn. Weekdays are a blur of commutes and meetings and deadlines, and even weekends tend to fill themselves up — classes, groceries, chores, errands — spinning like a top that never stops. But when you actually push your bike into the park and watch your kid wobble ahead, peering at everything, stopping at nothing in particular, you realize time suddenly goes soft. Soft enough to be tugged sideways for five whole minutes by a single sentence: "Dad, does that cloud look like a dinosaur?"

The bickering and chatter — that's just daily life. Along the way, he argues with me about why his bike can't go as fast as mine, why going uphill requires extra effort, why his helmet gets itchy after a while. I try to explain how gears work. He pedals off before I finish, shouting "I know, I know!" over his shoulder. He knows nothing, of course. This kind of failed communication happens every single day, and you never really get mad about it — because this is how a child talks to you. He doesn't care about the answer. He cares that you're willing to listen.

And the quiet steadiness — that's happiness. Halfway through, we pull over and sit on a bench by the path. He leans into me, his sweaty little head nudging my arm, and out of nowhere says, "Dad, let's come here again next time." Not a question. A statement. In that moment, it hits me: this is what happiness looks like. Sunlight, a gentle breeze, your child beside you, and the simple fact that he thinks spending time with you is a good idea.

Whenever I talk about cycling with kids, parents always ask the same question: what kind of bike should I get for my child? Here's my take: **safety first, and the right size matters way more than looks**. My little guy is currently riding the [Schwinn Koen Kids Bike](https://www.amazon.com/dp/B07K3G9XSV?tag=YOUR-TAG-20). The 14-inch model fits 3 to 4-year-olds perfectly, and the SmartStart geometry is a genuine game-changer for beginners — the seat sits lower, the pedals are positioned further forward, so kids can get on and off without struggling, and their feet can reach the ground with confidence. The brake levers are specifically designed for small hands, which means even kids with limited grip strength can stop safely. With over 6,500 reviews and a solid 4.4-star rating, it is easily one of the most reliable entry-level kids' bikes out there.

If your child is five or six, you might want to look at the 16-inch or 18-inch versions. The golden rule: both feet should rest flat on the ground. Don't fall into the "buy a size up so they can grow into it" trap — when it comes to learning to ride, confidence beats longevity every time.

![A young rider on a tree-lined path](images/middle.jpg)

On the tree-lined stretch of the path, dappled shadows dancing across the pavement, he suddenly slows down and looks back. "Dad," he asks, "when you're old and can't ride anymore, what happens?"

The question catches me off guard. I smile and say, "Then you'll be the one riding, and I'll be the one sitting on the back."

He thinks about this for two full seconds, then nods with deadly seriousness. "Okay. But you can't be too heavy."

We both nearly fall off our bikes laughing.

This. This right here is the stuff that ordinary days are made of — the moments that don't announce themselves, don't demand to be remembered, and might even slip your mind by Tuesday. But when you look back, these scattered fragments slowly gather and assemble into a map called "companionship." The afternoon he first rode without training wheels. The uphill slope where he fell, got up, and tried again. The look on his face when he said "let's come here again."

**The most precious company hides in the most ordinary days.** We spend so much energy giving our children the "best" — the best schools, the best toys, the best extracurriculars — and yet we forget that what they truly need is embarrassingly simple. A weekend with a big hand holding a small one. A slow, winding path with no finish line. A dad or a mom who is willing to listen to their nonsense.

The sun dips low as we wheel our bikes toward the exit. His cheeks are flushed pink, and his bike basket holds a pinecone he found on the trail, along with a leaf he declared "super cool."

"Dad, can we come next weekend?"

"We can. And the weekend after that."

He grins, satisfied, swings his leg over the saddle, and wobbles off into the golden sunset.

Ride together, grow together. The road ahead is long, and we are in no hurry at all.
